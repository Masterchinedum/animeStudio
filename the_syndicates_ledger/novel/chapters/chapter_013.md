# Chapter 13: The Blue District Sting

The Blue District did not sleep; it merely dissolved. Under the relentless, chemical-heavy downpour of Vesper City, the neighborhood’s towering blue neon arches bled into the slick asphalt, turning the narrow alleys into rivers of sapphire light. Above, the sky-high monitors of the financial district hummed with cold, corporate propaganda, but down here, the air tasted of fried grease, ozone, and wet wool. 

Ren Sato pulled his damp collar tighter against his neck. He walked with his hands buried in the pockets of his dark coat, his fingers lightly brushing the edge of his diagnostic slate. His mind was still processing the fallout of the last twelve hours. Yuki’s confession in the temporary safehouse still vibrated in his thoughts like a struck tuning fork. *An Apex recruiter.* The rival syndicate was moving pieces faster than he had anticipated, dangling a Tier-4 promotion in front of his junior analyst like a life raft. He didn’t blame Yuki for wanting out—everyone in Vesper City was looking for a way to climb out of the mud—but her exposure made her a liability. If Marcus Vance or Chairman Kang discovered she had even spoken to Apex, her demotion would be the least of her worries.

A low-frequency hum vibrated through the pavement as a cargo tram rattled along the elevated tracks overhead, throwing a cascade of dirty water over the rusted iron railings. Ren stepped into the shadow of a shuttered noodle stand, waiting for the spray to settle. 

He was three blocks from the transit hub, preparing to head back to the Sector 9 safehouse to check on Kenji, when the hairs on the back of his neck stood up. 

It wasn’t a sound. It was the sudden, deliberate shift in the ambient space behind him—the shadow that detached itself from the glare of a spinning neon sign.

"Don't turn around too fast, Sato," a voice said, low and gravelly, cutting through the steady patter of the rain. "The local precinct has three cameras pointing down this lane, and I’d prefer we don’t look like we’re exchanging pleasantries."

Ren froze. He recognized the tone before he saw the face. It was the dry, flat cadence of public law enforcement—the voice of a man who spent his days breathing police-station coffee and carbon-copy fumes. 

"Inspector Vance," Ren said quietly, keeping his hands exactly where they were. 

"Move into the alley," Jiro Vance commanded. "Slowly. Keep your hands where I can see them, or I’ll have a patrol unit sweep you up for loitering under the municipal vagrancy act. We both know how much the syndicate hates public records."

Ren complied, taking a deliberate step backward into the narrow gap between a brick warehouse and an old electrical substation. The air here was colder, smelling of wet cardboard and rotting cabbage. Jiro followed him, his heavy trench coat rustling. He didn’t draw a weapon, but his right hand remained resting on his hip, just beneath the flap of his coat.

In the dim light, Jiro Vance looked older than his brother, Captain Marcus Vance. Where Marcus was all sharp angles, tailored tactical gear, and polished cruelty, Jiro was weathered. His eyes were bloodshot, surrounded by deep, dark circles, and his jaw was covered in a rough stubble that caught the blue glare from the main street. He was Marcus’s estranged brother, but he carried the same stubborn Vance pride—only his was wrapped in a cheap, municipal-issue badge.

"I didn't think the VCPD spent much time in the Blue District," Ren said, his voice flat, devoid of the panic Jiro was likely searching for. "There's not enough corporate credit down here to justify the overtime."

"The VCPD goes where the rot goes," Jiro replied, stepping close enough that Ren could smell the stale tobacco on his breath. "And lately, every trail of rot in this city leads back to you, Sato. You and that forensic ledger you’ve been cooking for Kang."

"I am an auditor, Inspector. I balance books. If there is rot in the ledger, it is simply a reflection of the figures I am given."

"Save the corporate double-speak for the boardrooms," Jiro spat, wiping a stray drop of rain from his eyebrow. "I don’t care about Kang’s tax write-offs. I care about Director Han."

Ren’s eyes narrowed slightly. "Han is dead. A tragic suicide. The public police report was finalized three days ago."

"The public report was a joke written by my brother's PR team," Jiro said, his voice dropping an octave. "Han was Tier 6. You don't jump from a forty-story balcony because of a sudden attack of conscience. He was demoted, his assets were frozen, and then he was liquidated. I want his audit files."

"Those files are proprietary property of Kang Global," Ren said. "Accessing them without a syndicate-approved warrant is a Class-A corporate infraction. Even for the VCPD."

Jiro let out a dry, humorless laugh. He reached into his inner pocket and pulled out a small, laminated folder. He didn’t hand it to Ren. Instead, he flipped it open, holding it close to Ren’s face. 

It wasn’t a badge. It was a series of high-resolution surveillance photos. 

Ren's breath hitched, though he kept his expression entirely blank. 

The first photo showed a nondescript, low-rent apartment building in Sector 9. The second was a close-up of a young man with a slight limp, carrying a crate of rebuilt fuel cells into an alleyway. 

Kenji.

"Kenji Sato," Jiro read from a mental file, his eyes locked on Ren’s face, searching for the crack in the armor. "Tier-0. Erased five years ago after a supply-chain discrepancy in the outer docks. According to Vesper City Municipal Code Section 84, a Tier-0 citizen found within the restricted boundaries of Sector 9 without an active corporate sponsor is subject to immediate custodial detention. Minimum sentence is ten years in the northern extraction camps. Of course, most Muen don't survive the transport."

The rain seemed to grow louder, a deafening roar against the corrugated iron roof above them. Ren felt a cold drop of sweat trace down his spine, completely independent of the freezing rain. His brother. The safehouse he had spent half his life savings to secure. Jiro had him.

"You're outside your jurisdiction, Inspector," Ren said, his voice dangerously quiet. "Sector 9 is under Apex administrative control."

"Jurisdiction is a line on a map drawn by people who get paid a lot more than me," Jiro said, leaning in. "But the law is a flexible thing when it comes to the Erased. I can have a tactical sweep team at that Sector 9 address in twenty minutes. Or, I can forget I ever saw these photos. It all depends on how much you value your brother’s lungs."

The leverage shift was absolute, heavy, and suffocating. Ren had spent years building walls around himself, treating every relationship as a transaction, every person as a ledger entry. But Kenji was the one variable he couldn’t balance. He had designed the Erasure Protocol specifically to save his brother, and now Jiro was holding Kenji’s very existence over his head.

Ren forced his hands to remain still. He calculated the variables in milliseconds. If he gave Jiro the Han files, he would be betraying Chairman Kang and Marcus Vance. Marcus would realize the leak came from the audit department immediately. But if he didn't give Jiro the files, Kenji would be dragged into the black hole of the extraction camps, and a Tier-0 has no legal right to trial.

"The Han files are encrypted on a closed network," Ren said, his voice tight. "I can’t just pull them out of my pocket."

"You have twenty-four hours," Jiro said. He closed the folder with a sharp snap and tucked it back into his coat. "A drop point. The old municipal library on 4th Street. Leave a physical data drive in the return slot before midnight tomorrow. If the drive isn't there, or if the encryption is too heavy for my techs to crack, your brother gets a one-way ticket north."

Ren stared at the detective, his analytical mind clawing through the details of the trap. Something didn't fit. Jiro was a VCPD detective, chronically underfunded and isolated. He spent his time in the lower-tier districts, chasing street-level smuggling and small-time runners. How had he gotten his hands on Kenji’s exact location so quickly? Kenji had only been in the Sector 9 safehouse for a matter of days. The move had been handled with physical bearer chips, cash, and blind drops. 

"How did you find him, Jiro?" Ren asked, his voice stripping away the formal titles. "The VCPD doesn't have the satellite bandwidth to run facial recognition sweeps in Sector 9. Not unless someone paid for the server time."

Jiro’s eyes flickered, a brief, almost imperceptible hesitation in his confident posture. He adjusted his collar, looking away toward the mouth of the alley. "We have our sources, Sato. Just like you do."

"No," Ren said, stepping forward, his mind locking onto the discrepancy. "Sector 9 is a blind spot. The only way you get a precise coordinate registry like that is if someone handed you the active tracking metadata. A signature from a Kang Global internal security node."

Jiro didn't answer immediately. He took a half-step back, his hand returning to his holster. "I don't need to explain my investigation to you. You have your deadline. Use it wisely."

But Ren wasn't listening to the threat anymore. He was looking at the imaginary lines connecting the players. 

The tip hadn't come from a random street informant. It hadn't come from Apex, either—Apex wanted to recruit him, not burn his leverage. It had to be someone inside the Kang Syndicate. Someone who knew about his brother, knew about the Sector 9 move, and knew exactly which pressure points to press to force Ren to leak Han’s files to the police. 

*The Tailor.* Or someone operating under that shadow. 

By feeding Kenji to Jiro, the source was using the VCPD as a proxy to extract the Han audit files. If Ren complied, the files would enter the public domain, destroying Kang’s maritime networks, and Ren would take the fall for the leak. If he refused, his brother was destroyed. It was a perfect, elegant, and utterly ruthless pincer movement.

"The tip," Ren murmured, the realization crystallizing in his chest like ice. "It was anonymous. It came through an encrypted channel directly to your precinct's major crimes tip-line."

Jiro’s silence was all the confirmation Ren needed. The detective's jaw tightened, his eyes darting to the street as if suddenly realizing he was standing in the middle of someone else’s crosshairs.

"Twenty-four hours, Sato," Jiro repeated, his voice losing a fraction of its authority. He turned and walked out of the alley, his heavy boots splashing through the blue-tinted puddles before he was swallowed by the rain and the neon glare of the main thoroughfare.

Ren stood alone in the dark, the cold water dripping from his hair onto the collar of his coat. He was no longer just auditing a leak. He was auditing a conspiracy that had already written his own ending.
