# Chapter 9: The Family Dinner

The obsidian-and-glass sprawl of Chairman Kang’s estate clung to the cliffs of the Ridge like a dormant predatory bird. Below, Vesper City was a vast, glittering circuit board drowning in a sulfurous autumn downpour. Here, high above the smog and the neon rot of the Slag District, the air was scrubbed to a sterile, mountain-cold chill. 

Ren Sato sat in the rear of a armored corporate sedan, watching the rain-lashed security gates slide shut behind him. The private road had been cleared by three successive biometric checkpoints, each scanning his retinas and checking his Tier-4 clearance against the local node. 

He felt the weight of the analog micro-recorder tucked into the lining of his sleeve. It was a primitive, dangerous tool, but after his meeting with Sophia Thorne, he couldn't trust anything digital. *The blackmailer is using a physical terminal located inside Chairman Kang's personal residence.* The Custodian’s words echoed in his mind, rhythmic and heavy like the hum of the sedan’s electric motor. He was entering the beast's belly, and his only shield was a stack of fraudulent spreadsheets.

The doors of the estate opened to a cavernous foyer of polished slate and weeping water walls. A silent security detail, clad in the midnight-blue suits of Kang Global Private Security, escorted him through the house. The interior was a masterclass in oppressive minimalism—monolithic stone pillars, rare cedar screens, and glass partitions that overlooked the sheer drop of the cliffs.

"Mr. Sato," a soft, dry voice called out.

Chairman Kang Dae-Jung stood by a floor-to-ceiling window, a crystal glass of amber liquid cradled in his thick, scarred palm. He wore a traditional dark silk jacket over tailored trousers. At sixty-five, the patriarch of the Kang Syndicate possessed the physical gravity of a man who had personally survived three corporate wars. His eyes, set deep in weathered sockets, hummed with a cold, predatory intelligence.

"Chairman," Ren said, bowing precisely to the angle demanded of a Tier-4 subordinate.

"We have lost two directors in less than a week, Ren," Kang said, not turning back from the glass. His voice was a low rumble, devoid of anger but heavy with absolute expectation. "First Han, now Choi. The Ledger is hemorrhaging our most valuable assets. My board is whispering about systemic contagion. Tell me the fever is breaking."

"The audit is proceeding, Chairman," Ren replied, his tone flat, professional, and entirely rehearsed. "We are isolating the data anomalies. The perimeter of the leak is narrowing."

"Good." Kang turned, a thin, humourless smile touching his lips. "But business can wait for the dining room. My wife has been looking forward to your visit. She speaks highly of your... aesthetic precision."

A chill, thin and sharp, traced Ren’s spine. 

In the dining room, the table was a single slab of fossilized oak, long enough to seat twenty but set for only three. Mei-Ling Vance sat at the far end, silhouetted against the dark glass of the window. She wore a high-collared gown of charcoal silk—a Vance Haute Couture piece that managed to look both like armor and a shroud. Her hair was swept up, exposing the elegant, pale curve of her neck and the faint, pale shadows beneath her eyes.

As Ren approached, she offered a slow, formal nod. "Mr. Sato. It is a pleasure to welcome you to our home."

Her voice was steady, but Ren noticed the slight, nearly imperceptible tension in her shoulders. Her fingers were curled tightly around the stem of her wine glass. Beneath the table, out of Kang's line of sight, the silk of her gown rustled.

Ren took his seat opposite her, leaving Kang at the head of the table. The silence between them was thick, charged with the secrets they had traded in her private showroom and the digital ghost of the Erasure Protocol.

Silent, white-gloved servants began to serve the first course—a delicate arrangement of raw sea bream and winter radish. The only sound in the room was the soft clinking of silver and the rhythmic, muffled thrum of the estate’s localized frequency jammers.

"Mei-Ling tells me you helped her resolve a customs discrepancy with her winter collection last year, Ren," Kang remarked, lifting a delicate porcelain cup of sake. 

"A minor clerical error in the maritime shipping manifests, Chairman," Ren said, his eyes remaining on his plate. "The harbor authorities were using outdated ledger protocols. It was a matter of simple reconciliation."

"A simple reconciliation," Mei-Ling repeated, her tone light, almost playful, though her eyes remained fixed on Ren. "You make it sound so dry, Mr. Sato. To my designers, it was the difference between a successful season and a catastrophic write-off. My husband often forgets that even art requires a reliable foundation."

"Art is a luxury of stable systems, my dear," Kang said smoothly. He took a sip of his sake. "When the foundation cracks, the art is the first thing we burn for warmth. Is that not true, Ren?"

"The metric of survival is always pragmatic, Chairman," Ren replied.

Underneath the table, the toe of Mei-Ling's silk pump lightly brushed against Ren's ankle. It was a brief, deliberate contact. He didn't move. He kept his breathing steady, his gaze locked onto Kang. 

Mei-Ling reached for her water glass, her movements fluid and deliberate. As she did, she adjusted the heavy platinum ring on her right hand—a slow, clockwise twist. Three short rotations, a brief pause, then one final turn.

*Three, zero, one.* 

Ren’s mind, trained to spot patterns in millions of lines of code, cataloged the movement instantly. It was a room number. Or a security coordinate. *The terminal.* She was telling him where it was.

"You seem distracted, Mr. Sato," Mei-Ling said, her voice dripping with soft, dangerous irony. "Is the fish not to your liking?"

"It is excellent, Mrs. Vance," Ren said, meeting her gaze. "I am merely reflecting on the complexity of the systems we manage. Sometimes, the most dangerous variables are the ones we assume are dormant."

"A wise observation," Kang said, setting his cup down with a sharp click that made the silver ring. "But dormancy is a luxury we no longer possess. I received a report from Captain Vance this afternoon. He tells me your apartment was compromised. A transponder signal was detected."

The air in the room seemed to thin. Ren felt the cold sweat pooling at the small of his back. He had prepared for this, but hearing it from Kang’s mouth in this quiet, locked room made the danger physical, immediate, and suffocating.

"A minor security breach, Chairman," Ren said, his voice dropping an octave, adopting the rehearsed calm of a seasoned fixer. "A relic from Director Han’s personal files. It was a trap designed to trace the auditor. I initiated a physical purge of the hardware to prevent any external routing."

"And your assistant?" Kang asked, his eyes narrowing slightly. "The girl. Tanaka."

"She is under my direct supervision," Ren said instantly, perhaps too quickly. He forced himself to take a slow sip of water to mask the slip. "She is currently processing the clean data streams from a secure, air-gapped terminal. She knows nothing of the broader implications."

Mei-Ling took a slow breath, her hand returning to the table. Her fingers traced the rim of her glass, a silent warning to stay calm.

"I am glad to hear it," Kang said, leaning back in his chair. "Because the Board of Directors is growing impatient. They do not understand forensic accounting, Ren. They understand results. They understand bodies. When a Tier-6 executive hangs himself, they expect a culprit. When a Tier-5 executive is dragged out in cuffs, they expect a confession."

"The financial trail is clear, Chairman. It points to external manipulation. Apex Infrastructure is the prime suspect," Ren lied, keeping his face a mask of absolute certainty. He knew Sophia Thorne had told him the leaks were coming from *this* estate, which meant Kang was either testing him, or Kang himself was orchestrating the purge of his own board. "I am drafting the final report to implicate their offshore acquisition pools."

Kang watched him for a long, agonizing moment. The silence stretched until the hum of the rain outside seemed to fill the room. 

"Apex," Kang murmured, as if tasting the word. "Director Sterling has always been greedy. But greed is predictable. It is easily audited. What I fear is not greed, Ren. It is betrayal from within."

He looked directly at Mei-Ling as he spoke. She did not flinch. She met his gaze with the cold, practiced indifference of a woman who had lived in a gilded cage for a decade.

"Betrayal is a high-cost transaction, husband," Mei-Ling said softly. "The Ledger always demands its sacrifice."

"Yes," Kang agreed, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "And the Ledger always collects."

He tapped his fingers on the table, and the heavy oak doors of the dining room swung open. 

Captain Marcus Vance stepped into the room. He was in full tactical uniform, the polished black armor plates of Kang Global Security catching the soft chandelier light. His face was bruised from some unseen altercation, his eyes dark with a cold, triumphant malice as he looked at Ren.

Ren felt a heavy knot tighten in his stomach. He didn't look at Mei-Ling, but he could feel the sudden, rigid stillness that came over her.

"Chairman," Marcus said, bowing curtly. "The perimeter is secure. The additional security details have been deployed to the audit division."

Kang nodded, his eyes never leaving Ren’s face. "Excellent, Marcus. Sit. We were just discussing the progress of the audit."

Marcus did not sit. Instead, he stepped up to the head of the table, standing just behind Kang’s right shoulder like an executioner waiting for a signal.

"Mr. Sato is a brilliant accountant, Marcus," Kang said, his voice smooth, almost paternal. "But as I told him, we are no longer in a phase of quiet reconciliation. The threat is active. The fever must be cut out with a scalpel, not a pen."

"I agree, Chairman," Marcus said, his eyes locked on Ren. "The auditor’s methods are... slow. And perhaps too delicate for the current climate."

Kang turned his cup in his hands, watching the amber sake swirl. "Effective immediately, Ren, I am restructuring the audit’s operational hierarchy. You will remain the lead technical architect. However, Captain Vance is being promoted to directly oversee all security, data-recovery, and personnel decisions within your department."

The words struck with the weight of a physical blow. 

"Chairman," Ren began, his voice tight. "The audit requires strict analytical independence. Any security intervention could trigger the blackmailer's automated purge protocols—"

"This is not a negotiation, Mr. Sato," Kang interrupted, his voice dropping the polite veneer, revealing the absolute, iron-hard authority of a Tier-8 regional syndicate boss. "You have thirty-six hours to deliver the final Apex report. Marcus will be your shadow. He will approve your access, he will monitor your communications, and he will ensure that your junior analyst, Miss Tanaka, remains... focused."

Ren looked at Marcus. The captain’s mouth curved into a slow, mocking smile. 

With a single administrative stroke, Kang had stripped Ren of his operational independence, placed his brother’s safety in jeopardy, and turned his own department into a high-security prison. Marcus was no longer just a threat; he was his keeper.

Ren glanced across the table. Mei-Ling was staring down at her plate, her face perfectly pale, her eyes blank. But her fingers were pressed so hard against the stem of her wine glass that her knuckles were entirely white. 

"I understand, Chairman," Ren said, his voice hollow, cold, and utterly defeated. "We will begin the transition of oversight immediately."

"Good," Kang said, picking up his chopsticks once more. "Then let us finish our dinner. We have a long night ahead of us."

## FLASH FORWARD: CANON TRAILER
The rain-slicked concrete of Sector 4’s industrial canal was black as oil. Ren stood in the shadow of a decaying turbine housing, his coat soaked through, his fingers trembling as he held a cracked optical scanner. A few feet away, a figure in a heavy wool coat lay crumpled against the rusted iron grate of the drainage overflow, water pooling around a dark, spreading stain.

"You're late, Sato," a voice rasped from the dark.

Ren didn't look up as he pressed the scanner against the cold, dead thumb of the corpse. The device hummed, a single green indicator light flashing through the dark. "The security sweeps were tighter than expected. Marcus has three patrols on the bridge."

"He knows you're here," the voice warned, closer now. "He knows what you're harvesting. If you take that key, there's no going back to the tower."

Ren stared at the green light on the scanner. The face of the dead executive was half-submerged in the filthy canal water, but the Tier-8 biometric signature was already writing itself into his portable drive. "I stopped looking for a way back a long time ago."
