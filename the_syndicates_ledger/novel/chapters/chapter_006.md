# Chapter 6: The Interrogation Room

The sub-basements of the Kang Global Tower did not receive the high-society air filtration of the executive suites. Down here, beneath the concrete foundations and the high-voltage fiber-optic conduits that fed Vesper City’s digital veins, the air tasted of wet rust and old ozone. It was a cold, industrial dampness that seeped through the soles of Ren’s shoes and settled into the wool of his damp coat.

Captain Marcus Vance walked half a step ahead of him, his heavy boots rhythmically slapping the sealed concrete floor. Marcus’s tailored tactical coat was unbuttoned, revealing the matte-black holster of his sidearm and the small, gold-rimmed Tier-5 indicator pin on his lapel. To the security division, the pin was a license to erase problems; to Ren, it was a reminder of how quickly a man could be authorized to destroy another.

"The Chairman is not pleased with the pace of your audit, Sato," Marcus said, his voice flat, unaffected by the hum of the massive industrial HVAC units overhead. "Two days, two executives dropped to the dirt. Han is in the morgue, and Choi is sitting in a holding cell with no assets, no passport, and a very short memory."

"Auditing is not a physical sweep, Captain," Ren replied, keeping his voice carefully level, matching his stride to the soldier's. "When a ledger is compromised, the ledger leaves a trace. But if you rush the forensic reconstruction, the blackmailer deletes the target directory before we can trace the routing."

"We don't have time for your accounting theories," Marcus said. He stopped in front of a heavy steel door marked *Sub-Level 4: Secure Holding*. He swiped his biometric card, and the locks disengaged with a heavy, pneumatic hiss. "Choi’s wife hasn’t been seen since yesterday morning. He thinks she was taken by the same person who leaked his maritime logistics manifests. I want to know who that is before the Chairman decides we are all expendable."

The door swung open to reveal a narrow observation room. A sheet of thick, one-way smart-glass looked into a sterile, concrete chamber. Inside, bolted to a metal chair in the center of the room, sat Vice-President Choi. 

Or what was left of him.

Choi had been stripped of his bespoke silk suit, replaced by a coarse, grey industrial jumpsuit that hung loosely off his frame. His hair was disheveled, plastering his forehead with sweat, and his hands were cuffed to the steel ring of the table. The automated processes of the Ledger had already frozen his credit cards, revoked his corporate housing, and terminated his medical insurance. Within twelve hours of his demotion to Tier 1, the man who had controlled the largest deep-water docks in Sector 4 had been reduced to an administrative ghost.

"He hasn’t spoken a word since we brought him in," Marcus said, leaning his hands against the ledge below the glass. "He just stares at the floor. Go in there, Sato. Show him the numbers. Make him understand that his only path back to a Tier-2 pension is through us."

Ren adjusted the lapels of his grey suit jacket. He felt the cold weight of his own secret—the Erasure Protocol signature he had found in the code—press against his chest like a physical blade. If Marcus suspected that Ren’s own design was the key to this bypass, this room would be his own final destination.

"I need his local node access logs," Ren said. "And the raw transaction history of his maritime division for the last ninety days."

Marcus gestured to a terminal on the observation desk. "It’s already loaded. You have fifteen minutes before I start using more direct methods."

Ren sat at the terminal. His fingers moved across the mechanical keyboard with practiced, rhythmic speed. He bypassed the glossy corporate interface of the Kang Global network, diving straight into the raw, green-line system logs. He wasn't looking at Choi’s public transactions; he was looking for the micro-discrepancies—the fractional credit transfers that occurred in the milliseconds before the Ledger updated a user's Enforceable Influence.

Choi’s maritime logistics division had been bleeding. Not in millions, but in small, highly specific sums. 

*Twelve thousand credits.* 
*Eighteen thousand credits.* 
*Fourteen thousand credits.*

All of them were disguised as routine bunker-oil procurement fees, routed through a series of shell companies in the Sector 3 free-trade zone. Ren’s eyes narrowed as he traced the cryptographic headers of the destination accounts. The shells were registered under different names—*Blue Anchor Holdings*, *Vesper Tide Logistics*, *Meridian Import Escrow*—but they all shared a single, common clearing house.

*V-H-C Holdings.*

Ren’s heart gave a single, hard thud against his ribs. 

*Vance Haute Couture.*

The fashion empire of Mei-Ling Vance. The elegant showrooms, the silk dresses, the high-society charity galas where the city’s elite paraded their wealth—all of it was being fed by a quiet, systemic siphon of stolen maritime shipping funds. 

This wasn’t a simple blackmail scheme. Choi wasn’t just a victim of 'The Tailor.' He had been paying a regular tribute to an account directly linked to the Chairman’s own wife.

"What do you see, Sato?" Marcus’s voice cut through the silence of the room. The captain was watching him from the side of his eye, his posture tense, his fingers tapping a rhythmic beat against his thigh.

Ren’s mind raced through the permutations of the board. If he revealed the link to Vance Haute Couture right now, Marcus—Mei-Ling’s brother-in-law—would have to act. But would Marcus protect Mei-Ling, or would he use the information to destroy her? Marcus's private goal was to replace Chairman Kang and claim Mei-Ling for himself. If Ren handed him this weapon, he would lose all leverage over both of them. He would become a useless spectator in their domestic war, and useless assets in Vesper City were quickly erased.

"I see a pattern of structured extraction," Ren said, his voice remaining flat, devoid of any analytical excitement. "The leaks weren't just a sudden attack. Choi was being blackmailed for months. He was paying in small installments to keep his union strike records from being uploaded."

"To who?" Marcus stepped closer, his shadow falling over the terminal screen.

"A series of shell accounts in the Sector 3 free-trade zone," Ren lied smoothly, highlighting the entry for *Blue Anchor Holdings* while masking the clearing house routing code for *V-H-C*. "The encryption on the destination nodes is high-grade. It’s using a multi-signature key structure. I’ll need more time to trace the ultimate beneficiary."

Marcus stared at the green lines of code on the screen. He didn't have Ren's forensic training, but he had the hunter's instinct for hesitation. His eyes lingered on Ren's hands, which were resting quietly on the edge of the keyboard.

"You've gone quiet, Sato," Marcus murmured, his voice dropping to a low, dangerous register. "You always go quiet when you find a thread you want to keep for yourself. Just like you did with Han's accounts."

"If I give you unverified routing data, Captain, and you raid the wrong warehouse in Sector 3, the real blackmailer will know we've breached their network," Ren said, turning his head to look Marcus directly in the eye. "Then Choi’s wife dies, and our only witness becomes useless. Is that the report you want me to hand to the Chairman?"

The silence stretched between them, thick and heavy with unsaid threats. Marcus’s jaw tightened, the muscles along his cheek flexing as he calculated the risks. Finally, he straightened up and gestured toward the heavy door leading into the interrogation room.

"Get in there," Marcus spat. "Ask him about the shell companies. If he doesn't give you the key, I'm turning off the climate control in his sector."

Ren stood, smoothing his jacket once more. He stepped through the inner door, entering the concrete box where Choi sat. 

The air inside the room was even colder than the corridor, smelling of old sweat and fear. Choi didn't look up when the door closed. He kept his eyes fixed on the metal table, his shoulders hunched as if he were trying to pull his entire body inward, away from the light.

"Choi," Ren said quietly, sitting in the metal chair opposite the disgraced executive.

Choi’s hands twitched against the metal ring, the steel cuffs clinking softly. "They have her," he whispered, his voice dry and cracked. "They sent me a picture of her in a shipping container. She was wearing her blue coat. The one I bought her for her birthday."

"Who has her, Choi?" Ren asked, keeping his tone gentle, almost clinical. He wanted to project the safety of a professional, someone who dealt in numbers rather than violence.

"The Tailor," Choi sobbed, a single tear cutting through the grime on his cheek. "He knew about the docks. He knew about the manifests I signed five years ago. I thought... I thought if I paid, if I kept the accounts clean, he would leave us alone. But the Ledger... the Ledger doesn't forget. Once you put a secret in, it stays there. Waiting."

"You were routing the payments through Vance Haute Couture," Ren said, dropping his voice so low it was barely audible above the hum of the ventilation.

Choi’s head snapped up. His eyes, bloodshot and wide with terror, locked onto Ren’s. "No," he gasped, his chest heaving. "No, you don't understand. It wasn't like that. I didn't route anything. She... she was the one who told me how to do it. She said it was the only safe harbor."

"Mei-Ling?" Ren pressed, his fingers tightening against his knees under the table.

"She said her family's accounts were insulated," Choi whispered desperately, leaning forward as far as his chains would allow. "She said the Chairman never audited her fashion houses because he thought they were beneath him. She was trying to help me. She was trying to help all of us who were trapped under his thumb."

A cold realization settled into Ren's gut. Mei-Ling wasn't just a victim of the blackmail network. She was the clearing house. She was using her own business to wash the payments of the executives her husband was targeting, quietly building her own war chest of secrets and credits right under the Chairman’s nose. And now, she had drawn Ren into the middle of it.

"She’s lying to you, Sato," Choi hissed, his breath hot and smelling of copper. "She’s lying to everyone. She’s not trying to escape. She’s trying to—"

Choi stopped. His words caught in his throat, replaced by a sudden, harsh gasp.

His eyes rolled back, showing the whites, and his hands flew to his chest, his chains rattling violently against the table. His jaw locked, his teeth grinding together with a sickening click as his entire body went rigid.

"Choi?" Ren stood up, backing away from the table.

The disgraced executive’s legs kicked out, his boots drumming a frantic, desperate rhythm against the concrete floor. Foam, thick and tinged with pink, began to bubble from the corners of his mouth. His fingers clawed at his throat, his nails tearing into his skin as he struggled to draw a single breath.

"Marcus!" Ren shouted, turning toward the one-way glass. "Something's wrong!"

The door flew open, and Marcus Vance rushed into the room, his gun drawn, his eyes scanning the corners as if expecting an assassin to drop from the ceiling. 

"What did you do to him?" Marcus demanded, grabbing Ren by the shoulder and throwing him back against the wall.

"I didn't touch him," Ren said, his eyes fixed on Choi’s convulsing form.

The enforcers rushed in, but it was already too late. Choi’s body gave one final, violent shudder, his head cracking back against the steel chair, and then he went completely still. His eyes remained wide, staring blankly at the harsh fluorescent lights above.

Ren looked down at the table. Next to Choi’s hand sat a small, half-empty paper cup of water. It was the standard cup provided to all detainees from the dispenser inside the room—a dispenser that was locked behind a security panel that only the guard staff could access.

The water was perfectly clear. But on the rim of the cup, where Choi’s lips had pressed only minutes before, the paper had turned a faint, greasy yellow.

Ren looked at Marcus. The captain’s face was a mask of cold fury, but beneath the anger, Ren saw something else. A flicker of calculation. A sudden, quiet realization of what Choi's silence meant for the balance of power inside the tower.

"He’s dead," Marcus said, his voice flat as he looked at the corpse. He turned his gaze slowly back to Ren, his eyes dark with suspicion. "He died before he could tell you who the beneficiary was. How convenient for your audit, Sato."

Ren felt the cold air of the sub-basement fill his lungs, tasting of wet metal and death. He had his leverage over Mei-Ling now. He knew about the shell accounts, and he knew about her secret war. But as he looked at the dead man in the chair, he realized that the noose was already tightening around his own neck.

"The audit isn't finished, Captain," Ren said quietly, stepping around the body. "It's just become much more expensive."
