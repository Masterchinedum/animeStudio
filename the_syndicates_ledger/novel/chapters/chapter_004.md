# Chapter 4: The Slag District

The transition from Vesper City’s sparkling upper tiers to the Slag District was a descent through layers of damp, sulfurous air and dying light. On the elevated transit line, the polished chrome of the financial sector gave way to soot-stained concrete, then to the rusted iron girders of the lower industrial rings. By the time Ren stepped off the train, the rain had lost its clean, treated scent, carrying instead the metallic tang of heavy machinery and the sour odor of open drainage.

He wore a dark, unbranded stormcoat, his collar turned up against the drizzle. Here, his Tier-4 corporate ID card was worse than useless; it was a target. In the Slag District, the corporate grid ended, replaced by a patchwork of localized mesh-nets and pirate relays. The people who lived here were the grease in the city’s gears—unregistered workers, debt-refugees, and the *Muen*, those erased from the Ledger entirely.

Ren walked with his hands in his pockets, his fingers curled around a heavy, waterproof case of medical supplies. He navigated the narrow alleys with the practiced ease of someone who had memorized the city’s underworld before he ever learned to audit its boardrooms. Neon signs, flickering in languages the corporate offices had long banned, cast long, bleeding streaks of violet and amber across the oil-slicked puddles.

He turned down a subterranean ramp that led beneath a derelict turbine assembly plant. The air grew warmer, thick with the hum of low-frequency generators and the smell of hot solder. This was the underbelly of the Slag, where the discarded things of Vesper City came to be disassembled, rebuilt, or hidden.

He stopped in front of a heavy steel shutter painted with a faded corporate logo. He knocked three times, paused, then knocked twice more.

The shutter groaned upward six inches, revealing a pair of boots caked in dry grease. A moment later, the door slid up fully, and Kenji Sato stood in the dim, amber light of the workshop.

Kenji was older than Ren by four years, but the Slag had added a decade to his face. His skin was pale, mapped with fine lines of coal dust and old scars, and his left leg favored a heavy, mechanical brace that clicked softly as he shifted his weight. Once, he had been a lead systems engineer for a major shipping conglomerate. Now, he was a ghost, a Tier-0 non-person whose name had been scrubbed from every database in Vesper City.

"You're late," Kenji said, his voice a low, gravelly rasp. He coughed, a dry, rattling sound that shook his thin frame, before stepping back to let Ren inside.

"The transit lines were delayed at the border checkpoints," Ren said, sliding the heavy steel bolt closed behind him. He set the waterproof case on a workbench cluttered with stripped circuit boards, copper wire, and salvaged optical drives. "And I had to clear my tail before I crossed the canal."

Kenji eyed the case. "Did you get the inhalers?"

"Six months' worth of corporate-grade bronchodilators," Ren said, popping the latches. "And the broad-spectrum antibiotics you asked for. I had to route the purchase through a dummy pharmaceutical account in the third tier. It’s clean, but don't trade them all at once."

Kenji reached into the case, his grease-stained fingers tracing the sterile plastic packaging of the inhalers with a quiet, desperate reverence. "In the Slag, these are worth more than gold. The air in the lower sumps is getting worse. The filtration units on Sector 4 failed last week, and the wardens aren't planning to fix them." He took a deep breath, his chest rattling, and tucked two of the inhalers into his heavy work vest. "Thanks, Ren."

"How is the leg?" Ren asked, gesturing to the brace.

"It holds," Kenji said shortly, turning toward a half-dismantled delivery drone resting on a hydraulic lift. "As long as I keep the hydraulics greased. But that’s not why you’re looking at me like I’m a ledger balance that won’t resolve. What happened?"

Ren leaned against the edge of the workbench, his eyes scanning the dark corners of the workshop. "The audit is getting complicated. I had to destroy my secondary server last night. Kang’s security team ran a localized sweep. They were looking for the decryption signature of the files I took from Han’s terminal."

Kenji stopped his work, a soldering iron hovering an inch from a circuit board. "Did they trace it back to you?"

"No. Yuki managed to mask the outbound routing, but it was close. Too close." Ren paused, his voice dropping. "And there’s something else. The files Han was holding... they had a fragment of the Erasure Protocol embedded in the code. Not just a copy. A live, modified strain of the backdoor I built."

Kenji set the soldering iron down in its cradle. The warmth left his eyes, replaced by the sharp, analytical focus of the engineer he used to be. "That’s impossible. You kept the master architecture on an offline drive. The only way they could have a live strain is if someone extracted it from the Ledger's core node, or..."

"Or someone has been watching me since before you were erased," Ren finished. "I need to get you out of here, Kenji. The timeline is accelerating. I’m auditing Kang’s inner circle, and his wife, Mei-Ling, is playing her own game. She knows about the Erasure Protocol. She used it to threaten me."

Kenji let out a dry, humorless laugh. "Mei-Ling Vance. The fashion queen of Vesper Bay. You're playing with fire, little brother. She’s Kang’s prize, but she’s also a Vance. Her family built their empire on the bones of the dockworkers here. If she’s involved, she won't hesitate to use you as a shield."

"I know," Ren said. "But she’s also the only one who has access to the Tier-8 biometric keys. Her father's key is still active in the system's legacy vaults. If I want to trigger the protocol and clear your status, I need her."

Kenji shook his head, walking over to a small sink to wash his hands. The water came out a thin, rusty brown. "You’re worrying about biometric keys while the ground is crumbling under our feet. You need to worry about the sweepers."

Ren’s posture stiffened. "What sweepers?"

Kenji dried his hands on a rag that was more grease than cloth. "They came through the scrap market yesterday. Unmarked gray slickers. Not the local sector police. These were corporate security—Kang’s men, or maybe Apex mercenaries. They had handheld thermal units and active Ledger-linked sensors."

"Did they come here?"

"No, but they were asking the noodle vendor across the alley about a mechanic with a limp and a scar on his temple," Kenji said, his eyes meeting Ren's. "They had a digital rendering of my old face before the erasure. It was low-res, but it was me. Someone is looking for the ghosts, Ren. They aren't just cleaning up the active executives. They’re hunting down the loose ends."

A cold weight settled in Ren's chest. The sweepers weren't a random patrol; they were a targeted search. If Kang’s security forces were looking for Kenji, it meant Ren’s audit wasn't as secure as he had hoped. Or worse, someone had linked his forensic trail directly to his brother’s erasure five years ago.

"You can't stay here," Ren said, his voice flat and decisive. "If they have your description, they’ll trace the power draw from this workshop within forty-eight hours."

"Where am I supposed to go?" Kenji asked, gesturing to the cramped, metal-walled room. "I’m Tier-0. I can’t rent an apartment. I can’t cross the canal checkpoints without a registered biometric scan. If I step onto the main transit lines, the automated security grid will flag me as an unregistered anomaly in five seconds."

Ren reached into his coat and pulled out a small, black leather pouch. He set it on the workbench. Inside were three high-density bearer chips—physical currency, unlinked to any digital Ledger account, backed by private offshore gold reserves.

"This is eighty thousand credits in physical bearer chips," Ren said. "It’s half of my personal reserve."

Kenji looked at the pouch, his mouth tightening. "Ren, this is your escape fund. The money you’ve been laundering for the last three years to buy your own way out."

"It doesn't matter if you're caught before I can trigger the protocol," Ren said, pushing the pouch closer to him. "There’s a safehouse in Sector 9, near the old water reclamation plant. It’s run by a retired smuggler named Chen. He doesn't ask for IDs, and he doesn't use the corporate grid. He only takes physical chips. I’ve already paid his broker to secure the space, but you’ll need this to buy food and keep the local gangs off your back."

Kenji stared at the chips for a long moment. Slowly, he reached out and closed his hand over the pouch. "You're leaving yourself thin. If Kang freezes your accounts—"

"If Kang freezes my accounts, I’m dead anyway," Ren said. "Pack your tools. Only what you can carry in a single duffel. I’ll arrange for a private transport to move you to Sector 9 tonight. Don't use the main streets. Take the old drainage tunnels under the canal."

Kenji sighed, his shoulders sagging under the weight of his brother's insistence. "Always the accountant, Ren. Always calculating the margins of survival."

"Someone has to," Ren said.

Kenji turned toward a pile of salvaged electronics in the corner of the workshop. He reached behind a stack of rusted generator casings and pulled out a heavy, blocky device. It was a military-grade transceiver, its casing cracked and scorched, with several thick copper wires soldered directly into its main processing board.

"I found this three days ago," Kenji said, setting the heavy unit on the workbench. "It was in a scrap heap outside the Apex container terminal. The sweepers must have dropped it, or someone discarded it because the primary screen was shattered."

Ren looked at the device. The casing bore the distinctive, matte-black finish of high-tier corporate security hardware. "It’s a Ledger-linked comm-unit. These are encrypted with triple-layer biometric locks. It should be bricked without an active user's thumbprint."

"It was," Kenji said, a faint, technical pride flickering in his tired eyes. "The biometric block was hardwired into the main power rail. If you try to bypass it, the thermal charge inside the casing melts the storage chips. But they made a mistake in the firmware. They left a diagnostic port open on the secondary board."

Kenji flipped a switch on the side of the unit. A small, cracked LCD screen he had wired to the side flickered to life, casting a pale, cold blue glow over his face.

"I bypassed the battery block and routed the signal through an old television receiver," Kenji explained, pointing to a series of scrolling hexadecimal strings on the screen. "It can't send data—I don't have the cryptographic keys for that—but it can listen. And it’s receiving something it shouldn't."

Ren leaned closer, his eyes tracking the scrolling code. The data was structured in clean, high-priority packets, far different from the chaotic, noisy static of the Slag District’s pirate networks.

"Look at the header," Kenji said, tapping the cracked screen with a calloused fingernail.

Ren’s breath caught in his throat. The packet header didn't have a standard routing address. Instead, it bore a unique, high-tier cryptographic signature—a series of prime-number sequences that could only be generated by a Tier-8 transmitter.

"A Tier-8 signal," Ren whispered. "That’s... that’s Chairman Kang’s personal signature, or someone with equivalent clearance."

"It’s not just a broadcast," Kenji said, his voice dropping to a tense whisper. "It’s a ping. A real-time tracker, broadcasting an encrypted location coordinate every twelve minutes. And it’s not coming from the corporate towers uptown."

Ren watched the hexadecimal coordinates scroll past. He didn't need a map to recognize the grid sector.

"It’s broadcasting from the Slag," Ren said, the realization hitting him like a physical blow. "Right here. In the lower rings."

"Exactly," Kenji said. "Someone with Tier-8 access is down here in the mud, Ren. Or they left a live transmitter hidden in the scrap. And whoever it is, they aren't just listening. They're waiting."

The cold blue light of the salvaged screen reflected in Ren’s eyes. The audit was no longer just a financial puzzle or a corporate cleanup. The game had moved into the dark, wet alleys of the Slag, and the players were much closer than he had ever imagined.
